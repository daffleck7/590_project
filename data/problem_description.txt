UNIFORM ORDERING OPTIMIZATION — CFA Youth Soccer League

BUSINESS CONTEXT
CFA (Club Futbol Academy) is a youth soccer league in Orange County, California. The league runs four seasonal programs — fall, winter, spring, and a year-round competitive division (CFA). Each season the league president must commit to uniform order quantities before registration demand is observed.

The league processes roughly 2,000–3,000 orders per season through its Squarespace storefront. Uniforms include three product categories: jerseys (tops), shorts (bottoms), and socks. Each category is offered in up to 14 size codes spanning youth, adult men, and women.

CURRENT PROCESS
Order quantities are currently set by the league president using 25 years of operational intuition. This heuristic produces systematic errors in both directions: overstock ties up capital in inventory children outgrow, while stockouts trigger rush orders with premium shipping and delayed fulfillment.

THE JERSEY LIFECYCLE PROBLEM
Adidas jersey models (e.g., Condivo 22, Tiro 25) are produced for approximately two years before being discontinued and replaced. This creates a two-period decision structure with escalating costs:

- Year 1 overage: Unused jerseys carry to Year 2 at minimal holding cost.
- Year 1 underage: Rush orders at the same unit price but with added shipping costs.
- Year 2 overage: Excess inventory is sold on eBay at a loss (salvage).
- Year 2 underage: The model is discontinued by Adidas. Secondhand sourcing requires significant premiums, or the sale is lost entirely — unhappy parents and lost revenue.

The current jersey model (Tiro 25) is in Year 2 of its lifecycle — the final year. This makes the problem especially acute: any under-ordering this year cannot be corrected through normal channels.

PROBLEM CLASS
This is a multi-period, multi-product newsvendor ordering problem. The goal is to minimize total expected cost — combining procurement, holding/salvage (overage), and rush-order/stockout (underage) costs — subject to:
- A seasonal budget constraint (approximately $40,000 per season)
- Supplier minimum order quantity of 6 units per size
- Non-negativity constraints

COST STRUCTURE
All costs are expressed as a fraction of the selling price (unit_price).

- Purchase cost: 70% of selling price (wholesale cost to acquire one unit).
- Overage cost: 70% of selling price per unsold unit. Season-specific uniforms cannot be salvaged, so the full purchase cost is lost.
- Underage cost: 40% of selling price per unit short. This covers expedited shipping (inbound and outbound) plus a temporary jersey provided to the player while the rush order is fulfilled.

For the two-period joint formulation (see STAKEHOLDER MODIFICATION below):
- Year 1 underage: 40% of selling price (expedited shipping + temp jersey).
- Year 1 overage under joint model: 0% — unsold Year 1 stock carries to Year 2 at zero holding cost rather than being written off.
- Year 2 underage: 80% of selling price — the model is discontinued, so sourcing from the secondary market roughly doubles the shortage cost.
- Year 2 overage: 70% of selling price — full purchase cost lost on unsold discontinued stock.

The critical ratio for the single-period model is cu / (cu + co) = 0.40 / (0.40 + 0.70) ≈ 0.36, meaning the optimal policy orders to roughly the 36th percentile of demand.

STAKEHOLDER MODIFICATION — TWO-PERIOD JOINT OPTIMIZATION
Year 1 unsold inventory carries over into Year 2 at zero holding cost. Because the uniform model is discontinued after Year 2, Year 2 stockouts are especially costly (80% of selling price vs. 40% in Year 1). The optimizer should jointly decide Year 1 and Year 2 order quantities, recognizing that Year 1 surplus acts as a free hedge against Year 2 stockout risk. Compare the joint (Model B) solution against the independent single-period (Model A) solution to quantify the cost of myopia.

DATA AVAILABLE
Historical order data from 2020–2026, cleaned to 12,209 line items with columns: order_date, season, year, product_category, gender_age, size, quantity, unit_price.

EVALUATION APPROACH
Backtest mode: train demand models on 2020–2024 data, validate on 2025, and project the recommended order for 2026. The primary metric is total season newsvendor cost, not prediction accuracy (RMSE).

A 10–15% cost reduction vs. the baseline (historical average ordering) would be practically meaningful, representing hundreds of dollars per season.
