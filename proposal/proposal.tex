\section{Section 1 — Problem Statement and Business Context}

\subsection*{1.1 \; Business Context}

CFA (Club Futbol Academy) is a youth soccer league in Orange County, California, running four seasonal
programs (fall, winter, spring, and a year-round competitive division). Each season the league president
must commit to uniform order quantities --- across jerseys, shorts, and socks in up to 15 size codes ---
before registration demand is observed, processing roughly 2{,}000--3{,}000 orders per season through its
Squarespace storefront.

Order quantities are currently set by 25 years of operational intuition. This heuristic produces systematic
errors in both directions: overstock ties up capital in inventory children outgrow, while stockouts trigger
rush orders with premium shipping and delayed fulfillment. 

The problem is compounded by Adidas's jersey lifecycle. Each jersey model (e.g., Condivo~22, Tiro~25)
is produced for approximately two years before being discontinued and replaced. This creates a
\textbf{two-period decision structure} with escalating costs:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Year~1 overage:} Unused jerseys carry to Year~2 at minimal holding cost.
  \item \textbf{Year~1 underage:} Rush orders at the same unit price but with added shipping costs and fulfillment delays.
  \item \textbf{Year~2 overage:} Excess inventory is sold on eBay at a salvage price, incurring a loss.
  \item \textbf{Year~2 underage:} The jersey model is discontinued by Adidas. Sourcing secondhand stock requires significant premiums, or the sale is lost entirely --- resulting in lost revenue and unhappy parents.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{1.2 \; Problem Class}

This is a \textbf{multi-period, multi-product newsvendor ordering problem}. Each season the league
decides how many units of each size to order for three product categories before demand is realized.
The goal is to minimize total procurement cost --- holding, rush-order premiums, salvage losses, and
stockout penalties --- subject to a seasonal budget and supplier minimum order quantities. The
two-year jersey lifecycle adds a temporal coupling: Year~1 decisions directly affect Year~2 feasibility
and cost through carryover inventory.

\subsection*{1.3 \; Why This Problem Is Hard Without an Agent}

Three factors make this problem difficult without algorithmic support. First, \textbf{demand is
non-stationary}: youth players grow between seasons, so last year's size distribution is a biased
predictor of next year's. Second, \textbf{the cost structure is asymmetric and escalating}: minimizing
MSE is not the same as minimizing decision cost (Bertsimas--Kallus), and in Year~2 the underage penalty
can be an order of magnitude larger than the overage penalty. Third, \textbf{decisions are
combinatorially coupled}: every size order shares the same budget, so the optimal quantity for one size
depends on every other simultaneously. We expect the agent to outperform the current heuristic by
learning the cost-asymmetric demand distribution from six years of order history and recommending
size-level quantities that minimize expected total cost rather than prediction error.

After cleaning: \textbf{12{,}209 official game uniform line items} spanning 2020--2026, across 15 normalizedsize codes,

\subsection*{2.2 \; Additional Datasets}

\textbf{Registration data} (pending): player-level age, gender, and team records to enrich size-demand
prediction by connecting demographic features to sizing patterns. The league president has agreed to provide this data. 
\textbf{Adidas lifecycle data}: release and discontinuation dates for Condivo~20/22 and
Tiro~25/26 models, sourced from public retailer databases, used to label each order by lifecycle year.

\subsection*{2.3 \; Train / Test Split}

Chronological split: \textbf{train on 2020--2024} (approximately 9{,}500 line items across 8--10
seasons), \textbf{test on 2025--2026} (approximately 2{,}700 line items across 4 seasons). This
preserves temporal ordering and prevents data leakage from future seasons into training. The test set
includes seasons from both Year~1 and Year~2 of the current jersey lifecycle, enabling evaluation of
the agent's performance under both cost regimes.

\section{Section 3 — Methods Sketch}

\subsection*{3.1 \; Prediction Layer}

We compare three approaches with increasing decision-awareness (Bertsimas--Kallus, Lectures~2 and~4):

(1)~\textbf{PTO-Naive} --- MSE regression, predicted demand used directly as the order quantity (the
``wrong loss'' baseline);

(2)~\textbf{PTO-Adjusted} --- same MSE regression but order shifted by the newsvendor critical ratio
$q^* = \hat{D} + \sigma \cdot \Phi^{-1}\!\bigl(\tfrac{c_u}{c_u+c_o}\bigr)$, partially correcting for
cost asymmetry;

(3)~\textbf{End-to-End Prescriptive} --- a linear model trained directly on newsvendor cost via Adam,
with the gradient flowing back through the chain rule
\[
          \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_{\text{NV}}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}
          = \underbrace{\frac{\partial C}{\partial q}}_{\pm\, c_o / {-c_u}}
          \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\partial q}{\partial \hat{D}}}_{e^{\sigma \Phi^{-1}}}
          \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\partial \hat{D}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}}_{\hat{D}\,\mathbf{x}}
\]
allowing the model to develop intentional biases (e.g., over-ordering in Year~2 where underage costs
dominate). The Data Scientist Agent selects the best performer on holdout newsvendor cost, not RMSE.

\subsection*{3.2 \; Optimization Engine}

The constrained multi-product newsvendor is solved via \texttt{scipy.optimize.minimize} (SLSQP),
minimizing the SAA objective:
\[
  \min_{\mathbf{q}} \;\; \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{s} \bigl[c_o^{(s)}(q_s - D_{s,i})^+ + c_u^{(s)}(D_{s,i} - q_s)^+\bigr]
\]
subject to budget, minimum order, and non-negativity constraints. To handle potential non-convexity
in the multi-product case we use 15+ randomized restarts. The LLM-based agent generates solver code
from the \texttt{ProblemConfig}, adapting $c_o$/$c_u$ by lifecycle year and product category. KKT
shadow prices quantify the business cost of each binding constraint.

\subsection*{3.3 \; Constraint Handling \& Baseline}

Constraints are passed as \textbf{hard constraints} to SLSQP; a projection step maps any violated
solution back to the feasible set. The baseline is a \textbf{fixed-order policy} --- the per-size
average across training seasons --- approximating the president's current heuristic. The agent is
evaluated against this baseline on total season newsvendor cost (procurement + rush shipping + salvage
+ stockout), not prediction accuracy.

\section{Section 5 — Benchmark and Validation Plan}

\subsection*{5.1 \; Evaluating Your Own Problem}

The primary metric is \textbf{total season newsvendor cost} on the 2025--2026 holdout:
\[
  \text{Total Cost} = \textstyle\sum_{s} \bigl[ c_o^{(s)}(q_s - D_s)^+ + c_u^{(s)}(D_s - q_s)^+ \bigr]
\]
where $c_o$/$c_u$ vary by product and lifecycle year. We report: (1) average season cost reduction
(\%) vs.\ the fixed-order baseline; (2) stockout and overstock rates per size; and (3) incremental
gains from PTO-Adjusted over PTO-Naive and from End-to-End Prescriptive over PTO-Adjusted. A 10--15\%
cost reduction would be practically meaningful, representing hundreds of dollars per season.

\subsection*{5.2 \; Stakeholder Robustness}

\textbf{What is parameterized:} The full problem definition lives in a \texttt{ProblemConfig} JSON
schema --- decision variables, objective, constraints, and cost structure ($c_o$, $c_u$ per product
and lifecycle year). The Problem Formulator Agent parses natural-language modifications into this
config; any field can be reviewed and edited before the pipeline re-runs.

\textbf{Handled without re-engineering:} (1) A new constraint (e.g., ``add a sustainability cap of 500 total units'') $\rightarrow$ add to \texttt{constraints} list, re-run optimizer.
  (2) A shifted objective (e.g., ``maximize service level instead of minimizing cost'') $\rightarrow$ update \texttt{objective} field, regenerate solver code.
  (3) Changed costs (e.g., ``rush shipping now costs 2$\times$ more'') $\rightarrow$ update \texttt{cost\_structure}, re-run optimizer and explanation.
  (4) A new decision variable (e.g., ``also decide how many number transfers to pre-order'') $\rightarrow$ add to \texttt{decision\_variables}, may trigger solver code regeneration.

\textbf{Limits of adaptability:} The agent handles any change expressible in \texttt{ProblemConfig}
fields. A fundamentally different problem class (e.g., switching from inventory ordering to staff
scheduling) or a new data source not present in the existing dataset would require re-engineering of
the data and prediction modules. We will be transparent about this boundary during the Q\&A.
